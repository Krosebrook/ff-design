Frequently Asked Questions
IBM Institute for Business Value found that enterprise-wide AI initiatives achieved only 5.9% ROI on 10% capital investment. While individual workers using production-grade AI agents recover a median 6.4 hours per week (IDC/Microsoft 2025), that's ~16% of a 40-hour week — and not all of that translates to measurable business output. Values above 30% are optimistic ceiling estimates, not reliable baselines. Conservative models use 15–20%.
MIT's GenAI Divide 2025 found that organizations using specialized vendors to build and deploy AI succeed 67% of the time, while internal-only builds succeed only 33% of the time — even when the underlying technology and team capabilities are equivalent. The difference is governance framework, measurement baseline, and change management architecture that internal teams typically don't build because they're also running operations.
Deloitte's 2025 survey of 1,854 executives found that AI payback typically takes 2–4 years — significantly longer than the 7–12 month payback expected for standard technology investments. Only 6% of organizations see payback in under a year. This is because AI ROI is non-linear: the value compounds as prompt libraries mature, governance matures, and adoption velocity increases. Year 1 is investment-heavy; Year 2–3 is where compounding kicks in.
Burdened salary = base salary × 1.25–1.35 to account for employer taxes, benefits, office overhead, and HR costs. A $75,000 base salary costs the company approximately $93,750–$101,250 per year in total. This is the correct number to use when calculating the $ value of time recovered from AI — using base salary alone understates the ROI by 25–35%.
79% of organizations report data challenges impacting AI rollouts (IBM Q4 2025). If your data is siloed, inconsistent, or incomplete, the AI model cannot reliably process it — meaning your actual productivity gains will be a fraction of projections. At readiness 1/5, this calculator applies a 30% multiplier to your projected savings. At 5/5 (production-ready, governed data), it applies 100%.
Employees without AI upskilling represent approximately $8,700 in annual efficiency tax per seat — the productivity value they're failing to capture compared to AI-enabled peers (DataCamp 2026). The Cost of Inaction is calculated as: total headcount across all departments × $8,700 × your selected time period (1, 2, or 3 years). This is the cost of doing nothing, not a projection of AI ROI.
42% of AI initiatives show zero measurable ROI specifically because of measurement failures — not because the AI didn't deliver value (Gartner/ByteIota 2026). The value exists but can't be attributed without leading indicator baselines. If you skip adoption velocity, time-shift, and rework rate measurements in the first 6 months, you won't have the data to make a credible Year 1 revenue claim. Establish the baselines first, then escalate to P&L attribution.
Each department's multiplier reflects the proportion of their work that is AI-augmentable based on task composition research. Engineering (1.4×) has the highest lift because a large fraction of developer time goes to repetitive cognitive tasks (code review, documentation, debugging). Finance (1.15×) is lower because much of the work requires human judgment and regulatory accountability. The multiplier is applied on top of your base productivity gain % — it doesn't replace it. Source: departmental AI lift analysis from IDC/Microsoft 2025 and Deloitte 2026 enterprise adoption data.
